# Hypertension Reference Notes (RAG)

## Quick clinical grounding
- Hypertension can be asymptomatic; blood pressure measurement is essential for detection.
- Persistently elevated blood pressure increases risk of stroke, heart disease, kidney disease, and eye/vascular complications.
- Lifestyle interventions are first-line for many patients and include sodium reduction, physical activity, weight optimization, smoking cessation, alcohol moderation, and stress management.
- Medication classes commonly used include ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, and thiazide-type diuretics.

## Documentation cues for notes
- Include blood pressure values with units and context (clinic vs home readings, trend if available).
- Document adherence to medication and lifestyle measures.
- Record associated symptoms or absence of symptoms.
- Capture risk factors (age, obesity, diabetes, kidney disease, tobacco/alcohol use, family history when available).
- Provide clear follow-up plans and escalation instructions for severe symptoms.

## Threshold context (for grounding only)
- CDC patient education pages discuss hypertension as consistently at or above 130/80 mm Hg in ACC/AHA framing.
- WHO fact sheet framing commonly states hypertension at or above 140/90 mm Hg and emphasizes confirmation on repeated measurements.
- In practice, local policy and clinician judgment should determine final threshold usage.

## Safety signals to escalate urgently
- Severe headache, chest pain, shortness of breath, neurologic symptoms, visual changes, or markedly elevated BP with symptoms should trigger urgent evaluation pathways.
