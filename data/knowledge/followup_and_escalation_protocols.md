# Follow-up and Escalation Protocol Notes (RAG)

## Quick clinical grounding
- Plans should include explicit follow-up interval, home monitoring instructions, and escalation triggers.
- Return precautions should be symptom-based and easy to interpret.
- Documentation quality improves when plans are specific, time-bounded, and action-oriented.

## Documentation cues for notes
- Include when to follow up (for example 48-72 hours or 1-2 weeks based on severity and stability).
- State what to monitor at home (symptom trend, temperature, blood pressure, breathing status, medication response).
- Add emergency red flags requiring urgent care (severe chest pain, acute dyspnea, confusion, syncope, focal neurologic deficit, uncontrolled bleeding).
- Record communication method for follow-up (in-person/telehealth/phone) when known.

## Practical plan anchors
- For mild stable symptoms: conservative management plus clear monitoring and routine follow-up.
- For worsening trajectory: shorter follow-up interval and low threshold for escalation.
- For severe or high-risk features: immediate emergency referral instructions.
